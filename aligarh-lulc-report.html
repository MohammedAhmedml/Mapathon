LULC & NDVI Analysis Report

Aligarh District, Uttar Pradesh — Landsat 9 OLI-2

1. Introduction

This report presents a comprehensive Land Use Land Cover (LULC) classification and Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) analysis for Aligarh District, Uttar Pradesh, India. The study leverages multispectral imagery from the Landsat 9 OLI-2 sensor with a spatial resolution of 30 meters per pixel.

The primary objectives are to quantify the spatial distribution of major land cover classes, assess vegetation health using NDVI, and provide actionable insights for urban planning, environmental monitoring, and agricultural sustainability.

2. Study Area

Aligarh District is situated in western Uttar Pradesh at approximately 27.88°N, 78.08°E, covering an area of roughly 3,800 km². The district is characterised by a semi-arid to sub-humid climate, with the Upper Ganges Canal system providing the principal irrigation network. Key geographic features include extensive alluvial floodplains, canal-fed agricultural tracts, and the rapidly expanding Aligarh urban centre.

3. Methodology

3.1 Data Source

Landsat 9 OLI-2 multispectral imagery was obtained from the USGS EarthExplorer archive. Bands 2–7 (Blue, Green, Red, NIR, SWIR-1, SWIR-2) were used for classification, while Bands 4 (Red) and 5 (NIR) were used for NDVI computation.

3.2 NDVI Computation

The NDVI was calculated using the standard formula:

NDVI = (NIR − Red) / (NIR + Red)

NDVI values range from −1.0 to +1.0. Dense healthy vegetation yields high positive values (0.6–0.9), while water bodies return negative values and bare soil/built-up areas fall between 0.0 and 0.2.

3.3 LULC Classification

A supervised classification approach was applied using maximum-likelihood estimation with four primary land cover classes: Dense Vegetation, Moderate Vegetation, Water Bodies, and Built-up / Bare Soil.

4.2 Key Findings

  • Built-up / Bare Soil (34.5%) constitutes the largest class, reflecting significant urbanisation and exposed agricultural fallow around Aligarh city and surrounding tehsils.
  • Moderate Vegetation (32.0%) dominates the central floodplain area, corresponding to seasonal cropping patterns with wheat and sugarcane as primary crops.
  • Dense Vegetation (28.5%) is concentrated in the canal-irrigated northern belt, where perennial orchards and well-irrigated croplands maintain high canopy density.
  • Water Bodies (5.0%) account for the Upper Ganges Canal network and scattered reservoirs and ponds used for irrigation and aquaculture.

5. Applications

  • Environmental Monitoring: NDVI time-series analysis enables early detection of vegetation stress, deforestation, and wetland degradation across the district.
  • Urban Planning: The 34.5% built-up footprint provides spatial evidence for zoning decisions, infrastructure planning, and green-space preservation policies.
  • Agriculture & Sustainability: NDVI-based crop health assessments support precision irrigation scheduling and food security planning for the canal-fed agricultural belt.

6. Conclusion

The LULC and NDVI analysis of Aligarh District reveals a landscape undergoing significant land-use transition, with built-up and bare soil areas occupying the largest share of the district. The presence of healthy vegetation in the canal-irrigated northern region demonstrates the critical role of irrigation infrastructure in maintaining agricultural productivity. Continued satellite monitoring is recommended to track urban expansion trends and assess their impact on agricultural land and water resources.
